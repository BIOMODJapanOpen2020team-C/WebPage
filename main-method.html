Method

 Mechanism

We focus on the difference of a number of antibodies generated by the primary/secondary response. We designed a DNA-based reaction network mimicking the characteristics of the adaptive immune system.

 Initial solution

Fig.1: Structures and the concentration of the DNA complexes consisting of initial solution.


We assume that the system can be constructed in a sample tube containing a buffer solution. Initial solution consists of three complexes: Receptor (R), inactive B-cell (iB), and Fuel (F). As the concentrations of B and F are relatively higher than that of R, R acts as a threshold gate of the system.

 Primary response

Fig.2: Schematic diagram of primary response of DNA immune system.


(i) In the primary response, in the absence of Virus (V0), the functional components of the system remain in stasis. V0 reacts with Receptor R and forms a stable duplex RV0, releasing Signal S and Waste W0. This reaction is quite fast since R has a long toehold.
(ii) Triggered by S, B-cell changes its state from Inactive (iB) to Active (aB), exposing short toehold sequence.
(iii) Fuel (F) attaches the toehold and releases the Antibody (A) by strand displacement reaction. As the toehold is relatively short, this is a slow step compared to step (i). The difference of toehold lengths in (ii) and (iii) make the system possible to tune the priority of these reactions.
(iv) Addition of a fluorescent reporter for single-strand A allows the progress of the reaction to be monitored. The liberated A displaces a fluorophore-labelled strand from the detector duplex (iD), in which the fluorophore is quenched. The detector sequence is thus also based on the toehold-mediated strand displacement reaction, but with a longer toehold to ensure a significantly greater reaction rate.

 Secondary response

Fig.3: Schematic diagram of primary response of DNA immune system.


(v) Once the threshold of immune tolerance is reached in the system, secondary response is activated. V0 combines iB directly and forms V0iB, releasing A. Similarly as the primary response, A reacts with the fluorescence sequence which releases the signal.
(vi)V0iB moves on to react with F and releases V0 and waste (W2, W3). As V0 is recruisive, this can be regarded as a catalytic reaction and the generation of A is assumed to be very fast.

Fig.4 Expected result showing the time-dependence of fluorescence intensity.

As shown above, we can perform in vitro experiments to estimate the number of antibodies by comparing the fluorescence intensity (Fig. 4). However, since any such experiments cannot be performed in BIOMOD Japan Open 2020, we instead confirm it using simulation software such as NUPACK or Visual DSD.
