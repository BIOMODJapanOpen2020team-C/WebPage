 Initial solution

Figure 3-1: Structures and the concentration of the DNA complexes consisting of initial solution.

Initial solution consists of three complexes: Acceptor (A), B-cell mimicry (B), and Fuel (F). The concentrations of B and F are relatively higher than that of A, A acts as a threshold gate of the system.

 Primary response

Fig.2

Fig.2: Schematic diagram of primary response of DNA immune system.

In the primary response, in the absence of virus mimicry (V0), the functional components of the system remain in stasis. V0 reacts with Receptor R and forms a stable duplex W0, releasing signal S and Waste (W0). This reaction is quite fast since R has a long toehold. Triggered by S, B-cell mimicry changes its state from inactive (iB) to active (aB), exposing short toehold sequence. Fuel (F) attaches the toehold and releases Antibody mimicry (A) by strand displacement reaction. As the toehold is relatively short, this is a slow step compared to (i). The difference of toehold lengths between (i) and (iii) makes the system possible to tune the priority of these reactions. Addition of a fluorescent reporter for single-strand A allows the progress of the reaction to be monitored. Liberated A displaces a fluorophore-labelled strand from the detector duplex, in which the fluorophore is quenched. The detector sequence is thus also based on the toehold-mediated strand displacement reaction, but with a longer toehold to ensure a significantly greater reaction rate.

 Secondary response

Figure 3-3: Schematic diagram of primary response of DNA immune system.

Once the threshold of immune tolerance is reached in the system, secondary response is activated. Excess amount of V0 acts as a catalyst and trigger a much faster immune response on subsequent exposure to the same virus.
